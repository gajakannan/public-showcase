This can lead to faster development cycles and better alignment with domain boundaries. In a complex environment with multiple teams working on different parts of the system, this autonomy can be crucial. 4. **Operational Overhead**: Microservices come with operational overhead in terms of service discovery, fault tolerance, monitoring, and more. As complexity increases, managing a large number of microservices can become challenging. Modular monoliths, on the other hand, have lower operational overhead as they are deployed and managed as a single unit. 5. **Deployment Complexity**: With microservices, deploying changes can be more complex due to the need to coordinate changes across multiple services. In a highly complex system, deployment pipelines and automation become critical to ensure safe and efficient deployments. Modular monoliths simplify deployment as changes are typically made to a single codebase. 6. **Domain Complexity**: The nature of the domain and the business requirements can also influence the choice between microservices and modular monoliths. If the domain is highly distributed and consists of loosely coupled components, microservices may be a better fit. However, if the domain is relatively cohesive and changes are infrequent, a modular monolith may be more suitable. In conclusion, as complexity increases, it is important to carefully weigh the trade-offs between microservices and modular monoliths. While microservices offer benefits such as scalability, team autonomy, and complexity management, they also come with challenges in terms of operational overhead and deployment complexity. It is essential to assess the specific needs of the project, consider the long-term implications, and choose the architecture that best aligns with the goals and constraints of the organization.
